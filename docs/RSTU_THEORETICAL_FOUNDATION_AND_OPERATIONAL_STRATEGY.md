# RSTU Theoretical Foundation & Operational Strategy
## Grounding Tenant Power in Material Economics and Strike Capacity

**Document Purpose:** This document grounds the Reno-Sparks Tenants Union platform not in abstract principles or legislative hope, but in the material economics of rent extraction and the concrete power of collective rent strikes.

---

## PART 1: THE THEORETICAL FOUNDATION

### Why Legislation is Structural Theater

**The Core Problem:** Rent is not a policy failure—it's a power relationship.

Marx (1844) analyzed rent systematically:
- Landlords demand rent "for what is altogether incapable of human improvement" (natural fertility, location)
- Rent is extracted purely by possession of property, requiring NO labor, care, or productive activity from the landlord
- The landlord "endeavors to leave [the tenant] no greater share of the produce than what is sufficient to keep [them] alive and working"
- Rent is set not by what landlords "laid out" but by what tenants "can afford to give"—pure extraction based on control, not contribution

**Rent is legalized theft.** The landlord owns nothing they created. They extract what tenants produce.

**Why This Matters For Legislation:**
- A Nevada senator will not vote to limit landlord profits. Their campaign funding, real estate portfolios, and ideological commitment to "property rights" prevents this.
- Legislation assumes the landlord class will voluntarily surrender extractive power. They won't.
- Rent control is a concession, not a victory. It is only extracted through threat of collective action—not petitions or testimony.
- A senator won't act for 6 years. A landlord notices a rent strike immediately.

**The Historical Reality:** Every housing victory in history came from **strikes, organized withholding of rent, public pressure, and collective refusal**—not legislation. Legislation followed after power was already demonstrated.

### Why Strikes Work (And Why Tenants Win Every Strike)

**Strike Economics:**
- A landlord needs rent money. This is not optional—property mortgages, operational costs, and profit margin all depend on rental income.
- If 100 units in a building go on rent strike, the landlord immediately loses $50,000/month (assuming $500/tenant).
- A landlord can hire security, threaten evictions, issue notices—but they cannot compel payment.
- Landlords have ONE weapon: eviction. But mass evictions are slow, expensive, and create media attention (bad for business).
- A strike of sufficient scale forces negotiation within weeks.

**Why Tenants Always Win:**
- Tenants provide the labor (value) that the landlord extracts profit from
- Tenants can withhold that labor (rent payment) collectively
- Landlords cannot operate without rent
- The landlord's entire business model depends on tenant cooperation
- Strikes expose that the landlord needs the tenants more than tenants need the landlord

**Historical Evidence (From Research):**
- Rent strikes create visible, material wins (rent reductions, repairs, services restored)
- These wins create belief in other tenants ("I saw this happen, so it can work")
- Visible wins drive recruitment better than 800 social media followers
- One successful strike creates a proof-of-concept for the next campaign

### The Geographic Problem (And How to Solve It)

**Reno is NOT KC or LA:**
- KC Tenants operates in a walkable city where building-level organizing is geographically feasible
- LATU operates in a dense area where tenants know neighbors through proximity
- Reno is dispersed. A "building" might have 50 scattered units. Tenants don't know each other.
- Meeting attendance requires a 20-minute drive
- The relationship-building model (in-person meetings as primary) assumes geographic proximity

**But Strike Coordination is Geographic-Agnostic:**
- You don't need tenants to meet in person to organize a rent strike
- You need: communication tools, coordination, clarity on demands, and collective action timing
- Tenants can participate from home, coordinate via phone/text, and participate in the collective action (withholding rent)
- Geographic dispersion actually provides cover—landlords can't prevent the strike through surveillance of meetings

**The Solution:** The tool should enable **distributed strike coordination**, not in-person meeting organization.

### Why People Don't Show Up (And Why They Will for Strikes)

**Current Problem:** Meeting attendance is low because people don't believe change is possible. Legislation does nothing. Landlords ignore requests.

**Strike Solution:**
- When one building goes on strike and wins rent reduction, the neighboring buildings see it
- When members see concrete material improvement (repairs done, rent reduced, eviction stopped), they believe more is possible
- Strikes are not abstract—they're material events with visible outcomes
- Strike wins create recruitment momentum

**Psychological/Material Reality:**
- People participate in strikes for concrete benefit (lower rent)
- People tell others about strikes because the outcome is real and observable
- 20 families winning $100/month rent reduction spreads faster than 800 twitter followers
- Strikes answer the core question: "Can we actually change this?" with a yes

## PART 2: WHY THE TOOL MATTERS (And What It Must Do)

### The Operational Gap the Tool Must Fill

**Current Reality (From Operational Research):**
- Successful tenant orgs use spreadsheets + email for member/campaign tracking
- Communication is Action Network (email) + texts
- One-on-one organizer conversations are primary relationship-building
- Task assignment is verbal, follow-up is personal
- Member data is fragmented (organizer notebooks, spreadsheets, memory)

**At Reno's Scale + Geographic Dispersion:**
- A spreadsheet can't coordinate a strike across 5 buildings simultaneously
- Verbal task assignment doesn't work when you can't meet in person regularly
- Organizer knowledge (which buildings have grievances, who's been contacted, what's the temperature) must be recorded or it's lost
- You need to know: "Which buildings are ready to strike NOW? What are their demands? Who's the contact person?"

**The Tool Solves:**
1. **Strike Intelligence** - Map buildings by organizing readiness, grievance type, landlord, and tenant commitment
2. **Coordination Hub** - Members can see what building is organizing, what they're asking for, and how they can participate
3. **Task Assignment** - "Phone banking for Building X this Thursday at 7pm" with sign-ups, clear instructions, accountability
4. **Institutional Memory** - When an organizer leaves, the knowledge stays in the system
5. **Visible Wins** - Document strikes and their outcomes so other buildings see: "They did this, we can too"

## PART 3: WHAT THE TOOL IS NOT

### Avoiding the Movement-Death Trap

**From "How to Destroy a Movement" (decoded strategy):**

**DON'T DO THIS:**
1. **Keep things vague and directionless** → Unclear goals, no strategy, people get confused and leave
2. **Limit to slogans only** → No theory, no direction, no plans
3. **Aesthetic over action** → Pretty social media but no actual organizing
4. **Bureaucracy and discussion** → Hours spent debating process, no action gets taken
5. **Infiltrator paranoia** → Spend time vetting instead of organizing (real insurgency: build so fast they can't stop you)
6. **Celebrity leadership** → Movement depends on charismatic person, person leaves/is arrested and movement collapses

**What RSTU Must Avoid:**
- **Forums where people philosophize without action** → Every discussion thread leads to "what's our next action?"
- **Social media echo chambers** → Twitter followers don't show up. Use social media to ANNOUNCE wins, not to build membership
- **Leaderless democracy** → Consensus on every decision slows everything down. Have clear decision-makers (elected, term-limited, accountable)
- **Abstract principle debates** → Spend organizing energy on action, not theory debates
- **Meetings for meetings' sake** → Every meeting has clear action items and accountability

**What TO DO:**
- Clear, concrete goals for each building/campaign
- Transparent strategy (people know the plan and why)
- Action-oriented culture (we organize, we don't just talk)
- Decentralized execution (multiple campaigns happening in parallel, not all requiring central approval)
- Rapid iteration (try tactic, learn, adjust, try again)
- Visible wins (announce successes publicly and within membership)

## PART 4: THE ORGANIZATIONAL MODEL

### Why Decentralization Wins (And Why Size Doesn't)

**From "The End of Arrogance" (Curious George Brigade):**

**The Trap:** "Bigger equals better" (Statist/Capitalist logic)
- Large organizations require bureaucracy to manage
- Bureaucracy consumes resources and energy
- Net result: Massive org that does little actual organizing
- Hidden problem: Large org exists to perpetuate itself, not to win

**Better Model: Distributed Cells**
- Small, autonomous building committees (10-15 people per committee)
- Each committee has clear authority over their building's campaign
- Committees coordinate through simple infrastructure (the tool, regular check-ins)
- Citywide council coordinates strategy and provides resources
- Multiple campaigns running in parallel

**Why This Works for Reno's Geography:**
- Each building committee is geographically tight (neighbors, shared grievance)
- Committee members don't need to travel across the city to plan
- Communication is via the tool + occasional phone calls
- Campaigns move at their own pace without waiting for citywide approval
- One building's strike doesn't block another building from organizing

**Preventing Movement Death Through Structure:**
- No single leader = movement survives leadership loss
- Multiple active campaigns = visible, continuous activity
- Distributed accountability = organizers can't control all information
- Term limits on all elected positions = prevents entrenchment
- Public strategy = members understand direction, can notice corruption/co-optation

## PART 5: THE PLATFORM ARCHITECTURE (Operational Blueprint)

### Core Principle: Everything Serves Strike Coordination

**The tool has ONE job: Enable distributed rent strike coordination at scale.**

Every feature answers: "Does this help coordinate a strike or prepare the ground for one?"

### Section A: Member/Building Intelligence

**What Data to Collect:**
1. **Building Profile**
   - Address, property type, number of units
   - Landlord name/entity
   - Known issues (maintenance, rent increases, evictions)
   - Tenant contact list (with permission)
   - Organizing status (Uninformed → Aware → Interested → Committed → Strike Ready)

2. **Individual Member**
   - Building assignment
   - Grievances
   - Skills (translator, lawyer connections, media, organizing experience)
   - Availability/capacity
   - Preferred communication method
   - Pledge level (will they participate in strike? yes/no/maybe)

3. **Landlord/Entity Profile**
   - Properties owned (portfolio)
   - History (past evictions, violations)
   - Weaknesses (low occupancy, mortgage stress, public pressure vulnerability)
   - Past strike outcomes (if any)
   - Threat level (1-10 based on aggressive tactics)

**Why This Matters:**
- You can run queries: "Show me all buildings with rent increases in the last 6 months"
- You can target: "Which landlords own 5+ properties where we have members?"
- You can assess: "How many units are in strike-ready buildings right now?"
- You can plan: "If we hit these 3 buildings simultaneously, the landlord can't handle all three evictions"

### Section B: Campaign Board

**Campaign Lifecycle (Transparent, Traceable):**
1. **Intelligence Stage** - Building grievances identified, initial contact made
2. **Organizing Stage** - 1:1 conversations happening, demands being discussed, leadership emerging
3. **Commitment Stage** - Tenant committee formed, members publicly known to each other, demands drafted
4. **Preparation Stage** - Legal review, tenant agreements signed, communication plan set, strike date chosen
5. **Active Strike** - Rent being withheld, landlord negotiating, media engaged
6. **Resolution** - Agreement reached, terms documented, outcome posted

**What the Tool Shows:**
- Current campaigns and their stage
- Which buildings are in each stage
- Clear demands for each campaign
- Tenant committee contact info (encrypted/private)
- Timeline and next action dates
- Outcome documentation (did they win? what did they get?)

**Why This Matters:**
- Members can see active campaigns and join the one most relevant to them
- New organizers can see how previous campaigns unfolded
- Leaders can see which campaigns are stalling and need support
- Wins are documented publicly to inspire other buildings

### Section C: Task/Action Board

**What Members Actually Do:**
- Phone banking runs (recruit tenants in building X)
- Flyer distribution (Building X distribution route)
- 1:1 scheduling (next Tuesday, 6pm, building X apartment 3B)
- Document translation (Spanish, Vietnamese, etc.)
- Legal review (lawyer reviewing demands)
- Media coordination (press release for building X strike)
- Direct support (groceries for striking tenant family with kids)

**Tool Features:**
- Clear task description + deadline + skill required
- Sign-up system (who's doing what by when)
- Completion confirmation (was the task done?)
- Feedback (did the tenant show up? what happened?)
- Recognition (public thanks to volunteers)

**Why This Matters:**
- No one is confused about who's doing what
- Organizers can see capacity (is Juan overcommitted? pull him off some tasks)
- Accountability is clear but supportive (not punitive)
- New members see concrete ways to help immediately
- Tasks are VISIBLE so people feel like something is happening

### Section D: Strike Coordination Center

**When a Building Goes on Strike, This is the Hub:**
- Daily landlord demand status (has landlord responded to demands?)
- Tenant participation rate (% of units participating)
- Financial impact tracking (landlord is losing $X/day)
- Pressure campaigns active (media stories, public pressure points)
- Support network (money, food, legal help available to striking tenants)
- Communication log (landlord responses, negotiation progress)
- Media tracking (press coverage, social media reach)

**Why This Matters:**
- Strikers can see their collective impact quantified
- Other buildings see: "Building A just withheld $30,000 in rent and landlord came to the table in 3 weeks"
- Supporters know exactly how to help (fund food box, provide legal review, do media outreach)
- The strike narrative is controlled by the union, not the landlord's PR team

### Section E: Member Directory (Private/Encrypted)

**What's Available:**
- Contact info (encrypted)
- Building assignment
- Skills/experience
- Availability
- Communication preference
- "Strike Ready" status

**What's NOT Available:**
- Personal vulnerabilities
- Immigration status details
- Financial hardship details
- Personal address (only building)

**Why This Matters:**
- Organizers can quickly find "who has legal experience?" or "who speaks Spanish?"
- Members can find each other for mutual support
- Privacy is protected (landlords can't infiltrate and find vulnerable people to threaten)
- Capacity planning works (you know who you can call on)

### Section F: Playbook/Resource Library

**What's Documented:**
- Building organizing checklist (do this in this order)
- Demand-drafting template (what makes a strong demand)
- Legal protections (tenant rights, retaliation law, eviction procedures)
- Negotiation strategies (what to ask for, how much to ask for, escalation tactics)
- Direct action toolkit (flyers, media templates, petition language)
- Strike logistics (how to support strikers for 3 weeks, what people need)
- Tenant rights by scenario (late repairs, rent increases, discrimination, eviction notice)

**Why This Matters:**
- New organizers don't reinvent the wheel
- Consistency across campaigns (everyone using same demand template)
- Institutional memory survives organizer turnover
- Each building's campaign learns from previous ones

### Section G: Outcome Tracking & Public Stories

**What Gets Documented:**
- Building name, organizing dates, landlord
- Demands made
- Duration of strike (if applicable)
- Outcome achieved (rent reduction, repairs, eviction stopped, landlord sold building)
- Amount won (if applicable)
- Member testimonies (anonymous or credited)

**Public Accessibility:**
- Summary visible to all (prove victories happen)
- Full details available only to members (strategy details protected)
- Social media highlights generated automatically (amplify wins)

**Why This Matters:**
- Skeptical tenants see: "Here's proof strikes work"
- Recruitment happens naturally ("I want to join because I saw what they did")
- Media can cite actual outcomes
- The narrative is: "Tenants organized and won"

## PART 6: WHAT THIS SOLVES FOR RENO'S SPECIFIC CHALLENGES

### Challenge 1: Geographic Dispersion
**Solution:** Tool enables strike coordination without requiring in-person meetings. Members can participate asynchronously. Tasks are assigned digitally. Communication is clear and recorded.

### Challenge 2: Low Meeting Attendance
**Solution:** Don't optimize for meetings. Optimize for STRIKES. Meetings are preparation for strikes. Strikes are what matters.

### Challenge 3: Belief that "Nothing Changes"
**Solution:** Document and publicize every win. First strike win creates belief. Second win recruits people who saw the first. Third win accelerates.

### Challenge 4: Weak Relationship-Building (Can't All Be In-Person)
**Solution:** Relationships are built through SHARED ACTION in strikes, not just coffee meetings. Shared risk and shared victory create bond. The tool enables this at distance.

### Challenge 5: Volunteer Burnout
**Solution:** Clear task assignment with clear completion. Volunteers see impact. Distributed model means no one person is carrying everything. Recognition is public.

### Challenge 6: Organizer Turnover = Institutional Memory Loss
**Solution:** Everything is documented in the tool. New organizer can read campaign history and understand context immediately.

## PART 8: AVOIDING CO-OPTATION

### How Movements Get Neutralized:

1. **Bureaucratization** - Solve by keeping structure simple, term limits, clear authority
2. **Moderate Leadership** - Solve by democratic election and recall, no individual is indispensable
3. **Legislative Compromise** - Solve by maintaining strike threat, never claiming "we won" until victory is real
4. **NGO Professionalization** - Solve by member-funded (no grants that compromise), volunteer-led, democratic
5. **Police Infiltration** - Solve by cell-based structure (losing one cell doesn't compromise the whole) and rapid action (can't infiltrate something moving fast)

### Rule: If It Doesn't Serve Strike Coordination, Don't Build It

- No forum debates
- No philosophy discussions
- No vague "raising consciousness"
- No waiting for perfect consensus
- **Only:** Clear demands, clear strategy, clear action, visible wins

## CONCLUSION: THE TOOL IS A WEAPON

This platform is not a community hub, a social network, or a discussion forum.

**It is a strike coordination weapon.**

Every feature serves one question: "Does this help us win a rent strike?"

If the answer is no, it doesn't belong in the tool.

The user experience should feel like: **"Here's what we're organizing. Here's what I can do to help. Here's what other buildings have won. Now I'm ready to participate."**

Not: **"Here's a place to discuss housing policy."**

The difference is material and immediate.
